\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Goal of the project}

The objective of this project was to \emph{reproduce the results}
of the paper \emph{``Solving the Subset Sum Problem via Quantum
Walk Search''} (Lancellotti, Perriello, Barenghi, Pelosi; IEEE
Transactions on Computers, vol.~75, n.~1, 2026). The paper
proposes a gate-level quantum algorithm for the Constrained
Subset Sum Problem (CSSP) and provides a reference implementation
in \texttt{myQLM}; the task was to read the paper and the code
thoroughly, run the algorithm on an instance small enough to be
simulated classically, and verify whether the simulated output
matched the expected behaviour. When the output turned out to be
flat over all admissible solutions, the project shifted to a
structured diagnosis of the cause and to the production of the
smallest correctness fixes that could be agreed with the paper
authors.

\subsection{The paper at a glance}

The Subset Sum Problem (SSP) asks whether a subset of a given
multiset of integers sums to a target value $p$. The
\emph{constrained} variant (CSSP) further requires the subset to
contain exactly $k$ elements. SSP is NP-complete and underlies
several post-quantum cryptographic constructions, so it is a
natural target for quantum algorithms. The paper attacks CSSP
with an MNRS quantum walk on the Johnson graph $J(n,k)$, whose
vertices are the candidate $k$-subsets and whose edges connect
subsets that differ in one element. The walk is interleaved with
a sum-checking oracle and with a QPE-based approximate
reflection so that, after a number of outer iterations of order
$\sqrt{N/M}$ with $N=\binom{n}{k}$ and $M$ the number of
solutions, the amplitude on the marked vertex grows.

MNRS can be read as a generalisation of Grover's amplitude
amplification: instead of reflecting around the uniform
superposition over all states, it reflects around the stationary
distribution of a quantum walk on the graph. Because that
distribution is the unique $+1$ eigenvector of the walk operator
$U_W$, the corresponding approximate reflection is realised by
running Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) on $U_W$ and flipping the
sign of every component whose estimated phase is non-zero. The
formal cost of the algorithm is recalled in
Section~\ref{sec:paper}.

\subsection{Structure of the report}

Section~\ref{sec:paper} summarises the paper and the observed
behaviour of the unmodified reference code on a small instance.
Section~\ref{sec:work} reports the modifications applied to the
code, in chronological order, with the corresponding diffs and

the implementation, summarising the points that were addressed
and those that remain open. Section~\ref{sec:concl} draws the
conclusions.

\section{The paper and the original behaviour}
\label{sec:paper}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Problem statement}

Given a multiset $X = \{x_1,\dots,x_n\}$ of positive integers and
a target $p \in \mathbb{Z}$, the Subset Sum Problem asks whether
there exists $S \subseteq X$ with $\sum_{x \in S} x = p$.
The \emph{constrained} variant studied in the paper additionally
requires $|S|=k$. The candidate subsets are then exactly the
vertices of the Johnson graph $J(n,k)$, whose vertex set has
cardinality $N=\binom{n}{k}$ and whose edges connect pairs of
vertices that differ in a single element. A brute-force classical
search costs $O(N)$ oracle calls; the paper's contribution is a
quantum algorithm with the standard quadratic order
$O(\sqrt{N})$, but designed inside the MNRS framework so that
the explicit gate count and depth are smaller than a direct
Grover construction in the cryptographic regime
($n \ge 25$ for lower T-count, $n \ge 2^{13}$ for lower T-depth).

\subsection{The MNRS pipeline}

The paper builds the algorithm from four components.

\paragraph{(1) Johnson graph $J(n,k)$.} Each vertex is a
$k$-subset of $\{1,\dots,n\}$; two vertices are adjacent iff they
share $k-1$ elements. The spectral gap of the corresponding walk
admits the closed form
\[
\delta \;=\; \frac{n}{k(n-k)} ,
\qquad 1 \le k \le n-1 .
\]

\paragraph{(2) Marking oracle $U_f$.} Quantises the indicator
function of the solution: it accumulates the sum of the elements
of the current subset in an ancillary register and applies a
multi-controlled phase flip whenever the sum equals the target.

\paragraph{(3) Walk operator $U_W$.} The Szegedy quantisation of
one classical step of the walk on $J(n,k)$. It decomposes as a
double reflection,
\[
U_W \;=\; U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_{v_j})\, U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_{v_i}),
\qquad
U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_v) \;=\; U_U \, U_{\mathrm{ref}}(0) \, U_U^{\dagger},
\]
where $U_U$, the \emph{update operator}, generates the
superposition of the neighbours of the current vertex. A single
walk step therefore costs four applications of $U_U$ around the
two reflections around the all-zero coin.

\paragraph{(4) Approximate reflection via QPE.} The amplitude
amplification step needs a reflection around the stationary
state $|E\rangle$ of $U_W$. Since $|E\rangle$ has eigenphase $0$
under $U_W$ and the other eigenstates have non-zero eigenphase, a
QPE of depth $O(1/\sqrt{\delta})$ on $U_W$ acts as an approximate
reflection that flips the sign of every state whose estimated
phase is non-zero. The MNRS cost of the full algorithm is
\[
T_S \;+\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon}}\!\left(\frac{T_U}{\sqrt{\delta}} + T_C\right),
\qquad \varepsilon = M/N ,
\]
where $T_S$ is the cost of preparing the stationary state, $T_U$
the cost of one walk step and $T_C$ the cost of one oracle call.
The quadratic speed-up over a classical random walk lives in the
$1/\sqrt{\delta}$ factor: a small gap means a slowly-mixing chain
where the walk wins, while a gap of order $1$ collapses the
speed-up to a constant.

\subsection{What the paper validates and what it does not}

The paper provides closed-form analytical estimates of gate
count, depth and qubit count of every sub-routine; a comparison
against a Grover baseline showing better T-count for $n \gtrsim 32$
and better T-depth for $n \gtrsim 2^{13}$; and reference
open-source implementations in Qiskit and \texttt{myQLM}. It does
not, however, run the algorithm end-to-end on any concrete
instance: the cryptographic regime targeted by the paper is far
above any classical state-vector simulator (Table III of the
paper reports already 38 qubits for the smallest non-trivial
instance $J(4,2)$ with $m=2$), and the algorithm is designed for
fault-tolerant hardware. Validation is therefore restricted to
the building blocks taken individually.

\subsection{Running the code as-is on a toy instance}

The first experiment consisted in a direct execution of
\texttt{cssp.py} on the smallest instance compatible with
classical simulation,
$n=3$, $k=1$, $\mathtt{values}=[1,2,3]$, $\mathtt{target\_sum}=3$.
The unique marked solution is $S=\{3\}$, so an amplifying
algorithm should produce a distribution close to $(0,0,1)$ on the
vertex register $S_1$. Instead, the simulation returned a flat
distribution, identical to the initial Dicke state, after
approximately eleven hours of single-core CPU time on a circuit
of $28$ qubits and $\sim\!2\,500$ gates. Table~\ref{tab:baseline}
shows the direct run side by side with a run instrumented with

\section{Modifications applied to the code}
\label{sec:work}
% =====================================================================

The modifications below are reported in chronological order.
Every entry contains the rationale of the fix, the relevant
extract of the diff and the final measurement obtained after
applying the modification on top of the previous one. All runs
use the same toy instance $J(3,1)$ with target $p=3$, so that
the gate counts and the output distributions are directly
comparable. The full simulation logs are stored under
\texttt{documentation/quantum\_simulation/} (files 01--13) and
the corresponding commits are visible in the repository history.

\subsection*{Modification 1 --- Symmetric reflections A and B
on the full coin (17 March 2026)}

The walk operator decomposes as $U_W =
U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_{v_j}) U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_{v_i})$, with each
$U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_v) = U_U R_0 U_U^{\dagger}$ and
$R_0 = 2|0\rangle\langle 0| - I$ acting on the \emph{whole} coin
register $\omega = (\omega_1,\omega_0)$ of $n$ qubits. In the
original code both reflections were implemented asymmetrically:
Reflection~A controlled the phase flip only on
\texttt{wstate\_ones} via \texttt{Z.ctrl(k)}, and Reflection~B
did the analogous thing on \texttt{wstate\_zeros} only, with a
typo on the loop bound of one of the $X$-sandwiches. The fix
promotes both reflections to a multi-controlled $Z$ on the full
$n$-qubit coin and aligns the loop bounds.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- Before (Reflection B) ---
for j in range(n - k):
    prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])
prw.apply(Z.ctrl(n - k), qpe_s[qw_iter], wstate_zeros)
for j in range(k):                          # BUG: should be range(n-k)
    prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])

# --- After (17 March 2026) ---
for j in range(k):     prw.apply(X, wstate_ones[j])
for j in range(n - k): prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])
prw.apply(Z.ctrl(n), qpe_s[qw_iter],
          wstate_ones, wstate_zeros)        # control on the full coin
for j in range(k):     prw.apply(X, wstate_ones[j])
for j in range(n - k): prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
n=3, k=1, values=[1,2,3], target=3
{'nbqbits': 28, 'gate_size': 2581}
0.33333  |1>     0.33333  |2>     0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

This is a genuine bug: the original code does not implement the
double reflection of the paper. The output distribution, however,
is still flat: the fix is necessary for correctness, but on this
toy instance it is not the cause of the missing amplification.

\subsection*{Modification 2 --- Removal of the
$\alpha/\omega$ cleanup block (20--23 March 2026)}

Inside the \texttt{compute()} block of the QPE, after the
$\ell_s$ controlled walk steps, the original code performed a
manual ``cleanup'' of the ancillary registers $\alpha$ and
$\omega$ via \texttt{copy\_register(m).ctrl()} gates followed by
\texttt{generate(...)}. At that point those registers are not in
$|0\rangle$: they are entangled with the visited vertices.
Re-applying \texttt{generate} to a non-zero state acts as a
non-trivial unitary that corrupts the entanglement instead of
restoring it. The correct uncomputation is already provided by
\texttt{prw.uncompute()} at the end of the \texttt{compute()}
block (the standard AQASM compute/uncompute mechanism), so the
manual cleanup is both redundant and destructive.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- Before ---
for j in range(k):
    prw.apply(qregs_init.copy_register(m).ctrl(),
              wstate_ones[j], node_s_ones[j], alpha_ones)
for j in range(n - k):
    prw.apply(qregs_init.copy_register(m).ctrl(),
              wstate_zeros[j], node_s_zeros[j], alpha_zeros)
prw.apply(generate(k, 1),     wstate_ones)
prw.apply(generate(n - k, 1), wstate_zeros)

# --- After (20--23 March 2026) ---
# Block removed: uncomputation handled by prw.uncompute().
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
{'nbqbits': 28, 'gate_size': 1359}     # -47% gates
0.33333  |1>     0.33333  |2>     0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

The gate count drops by about $47\%$, confirming that the cleanup
block was redundant. A surgical per-element reset of $\alpha$ and
$\omega$ that does not touch the entangled state would be
preferable to a global removal, but its design lies outside the
scope of these patches and was therefore not pursued. The output
distribution stays the same.

\subsection*{Modification 3 --- Use of \texttt{insert\_lw}
instead of \texttt{insert\_ld} (22 March 2026)}

The paper presents two implementations of the update operator: a
\emph{low-width} variant that uses fewer ancillas and is
sequential, and a \emph{low-depth} variant that is parallel and
needs extra ancillas. In the reference code the \texttt{delete}
routine of \texttt{sliding\_sort\_array.py} was internally
calling \texttt{insert\_ld(...).dag()}. A reversibility check
showed that the composition \texttt{delete} $\circ$
\texttt{insert\_ld} is not the identity on a small input,
because of garbage left in the ancillas (a comment in the source
already flagged \texttt{insert\_ld} as ``not properly
reversible''). Since the walk operator chains many
\texttt{insert}/\texttt{delete} calls, the garbage propagates and
degrades the QPE step. The fix routes \texttt{delete} through
\texttt{insert\_lw(...).dag()}, which passes the reversibility
check.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- After (22 March 2026) ---
from .sliding_sort_array import insert_lw, insert_ld
insert = insert_lw if low_width else insert_ld    # reversible default
# delete() in sliding_sort_array.py now calls insert_lw(...).dag()
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
{'nbqbits': 28, 'gate_size': 1366}
0.33333  |1>     0.33333  |2>     0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

This is a structural fix: the walk operator was not unitary on
the ancillary subspace before the change. The output distribution
is again unchanged on the toy instance.

\subsection*{Modification 4 --- Clamping the spectral gap and
forcing a minimum QPE precision (27 March 2026)}

The closed-form formula $\delta = n/(k(n-k))$ is derived under
the implicit assumption $k(n-k) \ge n$. For $(n,k)=(3,1)$ it
returns $\delta=1.5$, which is a numerical artifact: a genuine
spectral gap of a reversible walk lies in $(0,1]$, and a value
greater than one signals that the formula is being applied
outside its intended regime. Plugging this back into the
precision requirement
$\ell_s = \lceil \log_2 (\pi/(2\sqrt{\delta})) \rceil$ gives
$\ell_s = 1$: a single QPE qubit can only distinguish phase $0$
from phase $\pi$, which is not enough to tell $|E\rangle$ apart
from any other eigenstate of $U_W$. As a defensive patch, $\delta$
is clamped to $1$ and $\ell_s$ is forced to at least three
qubits.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- After (27 March 2026) ---
delta  = min(n / (k * (n - k)), 1.0)
len_s  = max(int(np.ceil(np.log2(np.pi / (2 * np.sqrt(delta))))), 3)
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
{'nbqbits': 28 -> 30, 'gate_size': 1366}
0.33333  |1>     0.33333  |2>     0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

The clamp does not create a spectral gap that the graph does not
have: it only ensures that the QPE block is well-defined.
Instances with $\delta < 1$ are unaffected, so the patch is
backward-compatible.

\subsection*{Modification 5 --- Diagnostic checkpoints
(17 April 2026)}

To localise where the amplification is being lost, an
instrumented copy \texttt{cssp\_with\_checkpoint.py} was written.
It simulates the full state at six key points of one MNRS
iteration (Dicke + VBE, edge superposition, post-oracle, post-QPE,
post-diffusion, end of iteration) and prints, for each state with
$p > 5 \cdot 10^{-4}$, the probability and the complex amplitude,
parsed into the named registers (Dicke, $S_1$, $S_0$, $T$,
$\alpha$, $\omega$, QPE, Sum).

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
Ckpt 3 (Post-Oracle):
  Prob 0.0833 | Ampl +0.2887 | S_1:0 | Sum:2     (non-marked)
  Prob 0.0833 | Ampl -0.2887 | S_1:1 | Sum:3     (marked: phase flip OK)

Ckpt 5 (Post-Diffusion):
  Prob 0.0417 | Ampl +/-0.2041 | scattered signs
  (no concentration on Sum:3)
\end{lstlisting}

The trace shows two facts that are not visible from the final
measurement alone. First, the oracle is correct: at Checkpoint~3
the marked states have amplitude $-0.289$ and the non-marked
states have $+0.289$. Second, the failure sits inside the
QPE-based reflection: between Checkpoint~3 and Checkpoint~5 the
signs become evenly mixed, so the block that should rotate them
into amplitude is acting as a sign-scrambler instead. This
empirical fingerprint is the diagnostic of a QPE run with
$\ell_s$ too small to resolve the eigenphase of $|E\rangle$ from
the other eigenphases of $U_W$.

\subsection{Why the output remains flat after the fixes}

After Modifications 1--5 the marginal distribution on $S_1$ is
still $(\tfrac13,\tfrac13,\tfrac13)$. The reason is not a single
local bug, but a structural mismatch between the regime in which
MNRS delivers a quadratic speed-up and the toy instances that
are small enough to be simulated. On $J(3,1) \equiv K_3$ the
walker mixes in one classical step and the walk operator has
only three eigenphases; the value of $\delta$ obtained from the
formula introduced in Modification~4 falls outside the range of
a true spectral gap, and the precision $\ell_s$ collapses to a
single qubit, which is not enough to implement the approximate
reflection. On $J(4,2)$ the gap is exactly $\delta = 1$, again a
borderline case with no separation to exploit and a circuit that
already crosses the practical qubit ceiling of \texttt{PyLinalg}.
Genuine MNRS instances such as $(5,2)$, $(6,2)$, $(6,3)$ have
$\delta < 1$ and the algorithm should amplify the solution, but
the qubit count grows like $\Theta(n \log n)$ and quickly exceeds
the size of the simulator.

\subsection{The common pattern}

Reading the seven papers together, a clear pattern emerges: each
work that successfully validates its algorithm in simulation does
so by avoiding one of the three ingredients that, taken together,
make the simulation of our paper unfeasible. Some works
\emph{drop the walk altogether} and use a direct oracle followed
by amplitude amplification (Zheng et al., Sato et al., da Silva
et al.); others \emph{keep the walk but pick easy graphs} whose
spectrum is trivial or known in closed form, and either skip the
reflection or use a single walk step (Razzoli et al., Portugal
\& Moqadam, Wing-Bocanegra et al.); the only paper that targets a
genuinely hard problem at cryptographic scale (Perriello et al.)
follows the same path as Lancellotti et al.\ and \emph{gives up
on end-to-end simulation}, publishing resource estimates instead.

\subsection{Why the paper we studied is harder to simulate}

The pieces of the MNRS pipeline that make it powerful at
cryptographic scale --- the Johnson graph encoding, the
approximate reflection via QPE, the dependence on a small
spectral gap --- are precisely the pieces that prevent its
validation on a toy instance. They interact through three
quantitative constraints:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em]
\item the qubit count grows as $\Theta(n \log n)$, so the
smallest instance reported in the paper itself
(see Section~\ref{sec:paper}) already crosses the practical
ceiling of a state-vector simulator;
\item the spectral gap $\delta$ is greater than $1$ for the
small instances accessible to a simulator, which collapses the
speed-up factor $1/\sqrt{\delta}$ to a constant;
\item the QPE precision $\ell_s$ derived from the same formula
becomes too small to resolve the eigenphase of $|E\rangle$ from
the other eigenphases of $U_W$.
\end{itemize}
The other works either remove one of these constraints (no walk,
no QPE, no spectral-gap dependence) or operate at a scale at
which the simulator can still keep up. To the best of the
present literature survey, no published work covers the
combination ``MNRS + Johnson graph + small instance + classical
simulation + amplitude check''. The flat distribution observed
on $J(3,1)$ is therefore consistent with the literature, not
anomalous.

\section{Code-and-test mapping analysis}
\label{sec:mapping}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Method}

In parallel with the modifications, a structured comparison
between the paper and the implementation was carried out and is
recorded in \texttt{documentation/Code and test mapping.pptx}.
The mapping walks through every algorithmic block described in
the paper --- input preparation (Dicke state, vertex binary
encoding), update operator, marking oracle, QPE-based reflection,
diffusion, output measurement --- and locates the corresponding
sub-routine and tests in the code. For each block three questions
were asked: whether the implementation behaves as the paper
describes on inputs that we can simulate; whether the existing
tests in the repository are sufficient to validate that
behaviour; and whether the documentation makes it easy for a new
reader to match the code with the paper. The output of the
mapping is a list of items that were brought to the attention of
the corresponding author and, after agreement on which of them
were genuine issues, condensed into a single upstream pull
request. The remainder of this section reports the findings in
two groups: those that have been addressed and those that remain
open.

\subsection{Critical points that have been addressed}

The following items were submitted to the upstream repository
\texttt{paper-codes/2025-TC} as pull request~\#1 (branch
\texttt{correctness/mod1-mod4}) and are awaiting merge at the
time of writing.

\paragraph{Asymmetric reflections inside the QPE block.}
Both Reflection~A and Reflection~B now control the phase flip on
the full coin register of $n$ qubits, with the corresponding
$X$-sandwich applied symmetrically to both halves. This is the
content of Modification~1 of Section~\ref{sec:work}.

\paragraph{Degenerate QPE register on small instances.}
The lower bound on $\ell_s$ has been raised so that the QPE
register is never trivial; the change is invisible on instances
where the original formula already returned a value above the
new floor, and it is therefore fully backward-compatible. This
is the content of Modification~4.

\paragraph{Documentation gap between paper and code.}
The original \texttt{README} of the repository did not cite the
paper, did not list the dependencies and did not describe the
command-line interface of \texttt{cssp.py}. It was rewritten with
a citation to the IEEE TC paper, an explicit list of
requirements (\texttt{myQLM}, \texttt{qat-utils}) and a usage
block that matches the actual flags accepted by the script.

\subsection{Critical points that remain open}

A second group of items emerged from the mapping but lies outside
the scope of a small, targeted pull request. They are listed
here for completeness, since they are the natural next steps of
the project.

\paragraph{End-to-end validation is structurally infeasible on a
state-vector simulator.}
As recalled in Section~\ref{sec:paper}, even the smallest
instance reported in the paper exceeds the practical ceiling of
\texttt{PyLinalg} on commodity hardware. This is not a defect of
the code but a property of the algorithmic regime targeted by
the paper.

\paragraph{Surgical reset of the cleanup ancillas.}
The removal of the destructive cleanup block (Modification~2) is
a local mitigation: a per-element reset of $\alpha$ and $\omega$
that does not touch the entangled state would be the proper fix,
but it requires a different construction and was therefore not
included in the current pull request.

\paragraph{Test coverage of the composite walk operator.}
The existing tests cover the building blocks
(\texttt{insert}, \texttt{delete}, oracle, Dicke preparation) in
isolation, but there is no test that exercises the full walk
operator $U_W$ as a single unit on a small Johnson graph. The
bugs identified in Modifications~1 and~3 would have been caught
earlier by such a test.

\paragraph{Mapping of paper symbols to code identifiers.}
The register names in the code do not always match the symbols
used in the paper (for example the role of $\alpha_0$ versus
$\alpha_1$, or the two halves of the coin). A short
correspondence table inside the repository would significantly
shorten the on-boarding time of any future contributor.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:concl}
% =====================================================================

The project began as an attempt to reproduce the results of the
paper \emph{``Solving the Subset Sum Problem via Quantum Walk
Search''} on a small instance and turned, after the unexpected
flat output, into a structured diagnosis of the reasons behind
that behaviour. The diagnosis showed that the reference code
contained two genuine bugs (the asymmetric reflections inside the
QPE block, and a destructive cleanup of the ancillary registers)
together with one structural issue (a non-reversible variant of
the update operator) and that none of these bugs is responsible
for the flat output on $J(3,1)$: even with all the fixes applied,
the toy instance lies outside the regime in which MNRS delivers
its quadratic speed-up. The literature survey confirmed that no
related work succeeds in simulating an MNRS walk on a Johnson
graph end-to-end, and that the working alternatives correspond to
relaxing one of the constitutive ingredients of the algorithm.

The agreed correctness fixes were submitted to the upstream
repository \texttt{paper-codes/2025-TC} as pull request~\#1,
which contains the symmetric reflections, the lower bound on
$\ell_s$ and the rewritten \texttt{README}. The pull request is
under review by the corresponding author and is awaiting merge.
A patched local copy with all five modifications applied is
available in \texttt{code\_updated/cssp.py}, and the full
chronology of the changes is documented in
\texttt{documentation/modification\_report/}.

The natural continuation of the project would follow one of the
two paths suggested by the literature comparison: either a direct
Grover search on a compact vertex register, which validates the
\emph{idea} of amplitude amplification on small instances at the
cost of the walk speed-up; or a stripped-down MNRS variant on a
single register and an exact reflection, which keeps the walk
structure but is no longer a one-to-one reproduction of the
paper. Either route would close the gap between the
algorithmic regime targeted by the paper and the regime that a
laptop simulator can reach, which delimits any future work on
this codebase.
