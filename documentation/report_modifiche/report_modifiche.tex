\section{The starting problem}
% =====================================================================

The paper \emph{``Solving the Subset Sum Problem via Quantum Walk
Search''} (Lancellotti et al., IEEE TC, vol.~75, n.~1, 2026) provides a
reference \texttt{myQLM} implementation in the GitHub repository
\texttt{paper-codes/2025-TC}. The initial assumption was that this
code could be run end-to-end on a classical state-vector simulator
(\texttt{PyLinalg}) and that the solution would be visibly amplified on
the vertex register $S_1$. Instead, a direct run of \texttt{cssp.py} on
the small toy instance $n{=}3$, $k{=}1$, \texttt{values}=[1,2,3],
\texttt{target\_sum}=3 (chosen only because it is small enough to be
simulated) returned a \emph{flat} probability distribution
over the three admissible values. A second run, instrumented with
checkpoints at every stage of the circuit, shows that the circuit is
compiled and simulated without errors, but that the phase flip applied
by the oracle is not converted into amplitude by the diffusion step.

No run produces simulator errors: the circuit is built, linked and
simulated to the end. The problem is therefore not a low-level
implementation bug, but something either algorithmic or ``regime-related''.
The modifications listed in Section~\ref{sec:mod} were the sequence of
attempts to understand where the amplification was lost.

% =====================================================================
\section{The modifications (chronological order)}
\label{sec:mod}
% =====================================================================

The chronology below is reconstructed from the git history;
commits sharing the same underlying idea are grouped into a single
subsection. All final measurements were taken on the same
toy instance $J(3,1)$ with target $p{=}3$, to keep a homogeneous
baseline for the comparison.


% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Modification 1 -- Fixing the Reflection B logic
(17 March 2026)}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The paper defines the walk operator as a double reflection,
\[
U_W \;=\; U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_{v_j})\,U_{\mathrm{ref}}(a_{v_i})
\;=\; U_U U_U\,U_{\mathrm{ref}}(0)\,U_U U_U\,U_{\mathrm{ref}}(0)\,U_U,
\]
i.e.\ two symmetric reflections around the all-zero coin state. In the
original code the second reflection (``Ref~B'') was implemented in an
asymmetric way: the phase flip was controlled only on
\texttt{wstate\_zeros}, and the $X$-gates that move $|0\cdots0\rangle$
onto the target state were applied only partially. In plain words: the
\texttt{ctrl-Z} must fire when the \emph{whole} coin register is in
$|0\cdots0\rangle$, not only a part of it, and the $X$-gates must be
applied to both halves of the coin symmetrically.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- Before (original) ---
prw.apply(qrw_update.dag(),
          node_s_zeros, node_s_ones,
          node_t_zeros, node_t_ones,
          alpha_zeros, alpha_ones,
          wstate_zeros, wstate_ones)
for j in range(n - k):
    prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])
prw.apply(Z.ctrl(n - k), qpe_s[qw_iter], wstate_zeros)
for j in range(k):          # BUG: should be range(n-k)
    prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])

# --- After (17 March 2026) ---
prw.apply(qrw_update.dag(),
          node_t_ones, node_t_zeros,
          node_s_ones, node_s_zeros,
          alpha_ones, alpha_zeros,
          wstate_ones, wstate_zeros)
for j in range(k):
    prw.apply(X, wstate_ones[j])
for j in range(n - k):
    prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])
prw.apply(Z.ctrl(n), qpe_s[qw_iter],
          wstate_ones, wstate_zeros)   # control on full coin
for j in range(k):
    prw.apply(X, wstate_ones[j])
for j in range(n - k):
    prw.apply(X, wstate_zeros[j])
prw.apply(qrw_update,
          node_t_ones, node_t_zeros,
          node_s_ones, node_s_zeros,
          alpha_ones, alpha_zeros,
          wstate_ones, wstate_zeros)
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
n=3, k=1, values=[1,2,3], target=3
{'nbqbits': 28, 'gate_size': 2581}
0.33333  |1>
0.33333  |2>
0.33333  |3>
(runtime: 11h 37min CPU)
\end{lstlisting}

After the fix, Reflection~B matches the formula above. The bug is
real and had to be fixed for correctness, but on this toy instance
it is not what is hiding the amplification: the output distribution
is still flat.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Modification 2 -- Removal of the
$\alpha/\omega$ cleanup block (20--23 March 2026)}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

Inside the \texttt{compute()} block of the QPE, after the $\ell_s$
controlled walk steps, the original code performed a ``cleanup'' of
the ancilla registers $\alpha$ and $\omega$: a series of
\texttt{copy\_register(m).ctrl()} gates followed by
\texttt{generate(...)}. The original intent was to uncompute the
ancillas to preserve reversibility. The issue is that at that point
$\alpha$ and $\omega$ are \emph{not} in $|0\rangle$: they are in a
superposition that is entangled with the visited vertices. Calling
\texttt{generate} as if they were in $|0\rangle$ does not remove
gates, it adds them, and it destroys the walk coherence right before
the inverse QFT. The correct uncomputation is already provided
automatically by myQLM's \texttt{prw.uncompute()} at the end of the
compute block.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- Before (original) ---
for j in range(k):
    prw.apply(qregs_init.copy_register(m).ctrl(),
              wstate_ones[j], node_s_ones[j], alpha_ones)
for j in range(n - k):
    prw.apply(qregs_init.copy_register(m).ctrl(),
              wstate_zeros[j], node_s_zeros[j], alpha_zeros)
prw.apply(generate(k, 1),       wstate_ones)
prw.apply(generate(n - k, 1),   wstate_zeros)

# --- After (20--23 March 2026) ---
# Block removed: it was destroying the walk coherence.
# The uncomputation of the QPE ancillas is already handled
# by prw.uncompute() at the end of the compute() block
# (AQASM's standard compute/uncompute mechanism), so the
# manual cleanup is both redundant and wrong.
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
n=3, k=1, values=[1,2,3], target=3
{'nbqbits': 28, 'gate_size': 1359}     # -47% gates
0.33333  |1>
0.33333  |2>
0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

The gate count drops by about 47\%, confirming that the cleanup block
was indeed redundant. The output distribution, however, stays the
same: the bug was genuine but, on this toy instance, it was not what
was hiding the amplification either.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Modification 3 -- Using the
\emph{low-width} insert instead of the \emph{low-depth} one
(22 March 2026)}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The paper explicitly describes \emph{two} implementations of the
update operator (Sec.~III of the paper): \emph{low-width}, which uses
fewer ancillas and is sequential, and \emph{low-depth}, which is
parallel but needs extra ancillas. From the paper:
\begin{quote}
\itshape
``We report both a low-width and low-depth implementation of the
update operator. \ldots{} The low-depth strategy \ldots{} improves
circuit depth at the cost of additional ancillary qubits.''
\end{quote}
In the reference code \texttt{cssp.py} the \texttt{delete} routine was
internally calling \texttt{insert\_ld} (the low-depth variant). The
low-depth variant leaves some ancilla qubits in a non-zero state after
its \texttt{.dag()}, which breaks the reversibility of the update
operator $U_U$. The walk operator $U_W = U_U\cdots U_U$ is then no
longer unitary on the visible registers, and the QPE-based
approximate reflection degrades. The fix is to switch to
\texttt{insert\_lw}, which is clean w.r.t.\ ancillas.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- Before ---
from .sliding_sort_array import insert_ld
insert = insert_ld                     # leaves garbage in ancillas

# --- After (22 March 2026) ---
from .sliding_sort_array import insert_lw, insert_ld
insert = insert_lw if low_width else insert_ld    # reversible
# delete() in sliding_sort_array.py updated to use
# insert_lw(...).dag() internally
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
n=3, k=1, values=[1,2,3], target=3
{'nbqbits': 28, 'gate_size': 1366}
0.33333  |1>
0.33333  |2>
0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

The update operator is now reversible, as required by the MNRS theory.
This is a structural correctness fix for $U_U$, but it does not change
the measurable dynamics of the small toy instance: the output is still
flat.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Modification 4 -- Clamping the spectral gap and setting a
minimum QPE precision (27 March 2026)}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

\emph{Spectral gap in one line.} The spectral gap $\delta$ controls
how long the quantum walk has to run before a quadratic speed-up
appears: the MNRS cost scales as $1/\sqrt{\varepsilon\,\delta}$, with
$\varepsilon$ the fraction of marked vertices. A small $\delta$ means
the walk needs many steps to mix, and MNRS wins against brute force;
a $\delta$ close to $1$ means the walk mixes in a handful of steps, so
$1/\sqrt{\delta} = O(1)$ and there is no quadratic regime to observe.

The paper uses the Johnson-graph formula
\[
\delta \;=\; \frac{n}{k(n-k)},
\qquad
\ell_s \;=\; \left\lceil \log_2\!\frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{\delta}} \right\rceil,
\]
which is exact for $J(n,k)$, but designed for instances with
$k(n-k)\ge n$. For the toy instance $(n,k)=(3,1)$ one gets
$\delta=1.5$ and $\ell_s=1$: a single QPE qubit is not enough to
resolve the phase of $|E\rangle$ from the other eigenphases of $U_W$,
so the block that is supposed to implement an approximate reflection
does not act as a reflection at all. As a defensive patch, $\delta$
is clamped to $1$ and $\ell_s$ is forced to at least $3$ qubits.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# --- Before ---
delta  = n / (k * (n - k))
len_s  = int(np.ceil(np.log2(np.pi / (2 * np.sqrt(delta)))))

# --- After (27 March 2026) ---
# The paper formula is designed for k*(n-k) >= n; for smaller
# instances 1/sqrt(delta) becomes O(1) and the QPE block is
# degenerate. Clamp delta to 1 so that the rest of the code
# does not produce negative len_s.
delta  = min(n / (k * (n - k)), 1.0)
# A single QPE qubit only distinguishes phase 0 from pi,
# which is not enough to approximate a reflection.
len_s  = max(int(np.ceil(np.log2(np.pi / (2 * np.sqrt(delta))))), 3)
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
n=3, k=1, values=[1,2,3], target=3
{'nbqbits': 28 -> 30, 'gate_size': 1366}
0.33333  |1>
0.33333  |2>
0.33333  |3>
\end{lstlisting}

The QPE precision goes from 1 to 3 qubits, but the output is still
flat. The clamp is only a numerical safeguard: it does not create a
spectral gap that the toy graph does not have.

\subsection*{Modification 5 -- Checkpoint debugger for amplitude
inspection (17 April 2026)}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

To find out \emph{where} the amplification is lost, an instrumented
copy \texttt{cssp\_with\_checkpoint.py} was written. It simulates the
full state at six key points of the circuit (Dicke+VBE, edge
superposition, post-oracle, post-QPE, post-diffusion, end of
iteration). At every checkpoint it prints probability and complex
amplitude of every state with $p>5\cdot 10^{-4}$, parsing the raw
bitstring into the named registers (Dicke, $S_1$, $S_0$, $T_1$,
$T_0$, $\alpha$, $\omega$, QPE, Sum).

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
def simulate_and_print_state(prw, n, k, m, len_s, n_qubits_sum, label):
    circ = prw.to_circ(link=[classarith, cuccaro_arith])
    res  = PyLinalg().submit(circ.to_job())
    for sample in res:
        if sample.probability > 5e-4:
            raw = str(sample.state)[1:-1]
            # ... parse raw into dicke / S_1 / S_0 / t_* / a_* / w_* / qpe / sum ...
            print(f"Prob: {sample.probability:.5f} "
                  f"| Ampl: {sample.amplitude:+.4f}"
                  f"| S_1:{s1_str} | Sum:{sum_str}")
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=plain]
Ckpt 2 (Edge Superposition):
  Prob 0.0833 | Ampl +0.2887 | S_1:0 | Sum:2     (non-marked)
  Prob 0.0833 | Ampl +0.2887 | S_1:1 | Sum:3     (marked)   x 8 states

Ckpt 3 (Post-Oracle):
  Prob 0.0833 | Ampl +0.2887 | S_1:0 | Sum:2
  Prob 0.0833 | Ampl -0.2887 | S_1:1 | Sum:3     <-- phase flip OK

Ckpt 5 (Post-Diffusion):
  Prob 0.0417 ... Ampl +/-0.2041 scattered
  (no concentration on Sum:3)

Ckpt 6 (End of iteration):
  Prob 0.0833 ... Ampl +/-0.2887 signs mixed across S_1=0 and S_1=1
\end{lstlisting}

This modification does not touch the algorithm, but it was decisive
for the diagnosis: it shows empirically that \emph{the oracle works}
(the phase flip appears on the marked states at Checkpoint~3) and that
the problem appears between Checkpoint~3 and Checkpoint~5, i.e.\
inside the QPE-based approximate reflection.

\emph{Why this matters and what it implies.} At Checkpoint~3 the
circuit is in exactly the state that a textbook Grover diffusion
would now rotate into an amplitude peak: non-marked states have
amplitude $+0.289$, marked states have $-0.289$. What the approximate
reflection $U_{\mathrm{ref}}(E)$ is supposed to do is act as $+I$ on
the stationary walk state $|E\rangle$ and as $-I$ on its orthogonal
complement, producing a net rotation that concentrates probability on
the marked subspace. In practice we see the signs become mixed and
evenly distributed, i.e.\ the reflection is acting like a random
sign-scrambler rather than a reflection. This is the fingerprint of a
QPE run with $\ell_s$ too small to resolve the phase of $|E\rangle$
from the other eigenphases of $U_W$, which on $J(3,1)$ is unavoidable
because the walk mixes in $O(1)$ steps and $1/\sqrt{\delta}=O(1)$: no
further patch inside the QPE block will fix the result on this
instance, the regime itself lies outside the one MNRS is built for.

\section{Why it does not work: the final diagnosis}
\label{sec:diag}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Conclusions}

After the modifications in Section~\ref{sec:mod} the final distribution
on $S_1$ is still exactly $(\tfrac13,\tfrac13,\tfrac13)$. The reason is
not a single local bug, but a mismatch between the regime in which
MNRS is designed to work and the toy instances chosen for the
experiments. The instances $J(3,1)$ and $J(4,2)$
used during debugging were picked as deliberately \emph{small} examples,
so that the full state vector could be simulated on a laptop. They are
not representative of the instances the paper targets, and their job
was only to make the pipeline runnable.

\paragraph{Case 1 -- $J(3,1)$ (the toy example used for debugging).}
Intuitively: on $J(3,1)$ the Johnson graph is just the triangle $K_3$,
three vertices all connected to each other. A walker on a triangle
mixes in one step, so there is no ``slow direction'' in the walk that
a QPE with $\ell_s=1$ qubit can detect. Plugging $(n,k)=(3,1)$ into
the paper formula gives $\delta=3/(1\cdot 2)=1.5$ and, from the MNRS
cost scaling $1/\sqrt{\varepsilon\,\delta}$ with $\varepsilon=1/3$,
a total of about two ``amplification'' iterations of a one-step walk:
the quantum-walk speed-up collapses to $O(1)$ and the run essentially
reduces to a single oracle plus a reflection, i.e.\ there is no
quadratic regime to observe. In practice no tuning inside the QPE
block (precision $\ell_s$, inverse or direct QFT, scaled powers
$U_W^{2^j}$, symmetric Reflection~B) can produce a non-uniform
distribution on $S_1$ here: forcing $\ell_s$ by hand does not bring
back a gap that the graph does not have, and the approximate
reflection cannot act as the textbook $+I$ on $|E\rangle$ / $-I$ on
the complement when only one or two eigenphases can be resolved.

\paragraph{Case 2 -- $J(4,2)$.}
For $(n,k)=(4,2)$ we have $\delta = 4/(2\cdot 2) = 1$ exactly, a
borderline case. The algorithm is formally defined, but there is no
spectral separation to exploit: even with $\ell_s=3$--$5$ and the
scaled powers $U_W^{2^j}$ one gets only a marginal amplification, and
with just $\binom{4}{2}=6$ vertices a direct Grover search on the
vertex register is simpler and at least as effective in practice.

\paragraph{Case 3 -- Genuine $J(n,k)$ with $\delta<1$.}
For instances such as $(5,2)$, $(6,2)$, $(6,3)$ the spectral gap is
strictly below $1$ and MNRS is in its native regime: with the fixes of
Section~\ref{sec:mod} the algorithm should amplify the solution. The
obstacle here is purely computational (see
Section~\ref{subsec:cause}): the qubit count grows like
$\Theta(n\log n)$ and the state-vector simulator cannot follow. In
other words, $J(3,1)$ and $J(4,2)$ were chosen because they are
simulable, not because they satisfy the theoretical preconditions of
the algorithm; the instances where the algorithm does work are out of
reach for a laptop simulator.

\subsection{Distinct causes}
\label{subsec:cause}

It is important to separate three causes that look like the same
symptom on a flat output distribution but are actually independent.
The first is a purely \emph{physical} limit of classical simulation:
already at $(3,1)$ the circuit uses 28 qubits (Dicke, $S_1$, $S_0$,
$T_1$, $T_0$, $\alpha_1$, $\alpha_0$, $\omega_1$, $\omega_0$, QPE and
Sum registers), and \texttt{PyLinalg} is a full state-vector
simulator, so every gate touches a vector of $2^{28}\approx 2.7\cdot
10^8$ complex amplitudes; with about 2500 gates per iteration a single
run already lasts several hours on a single core. Growing $(n,k)$ into
the regime $\delta<1$ pushes the qubit count above 35, multiplying the
state-vector size by $\sim 128$ and making the simulation unfeasible. The second cause is a \emph{structural} limit of MNRS on
trivial instances: the algorithm requires $\delta<1$ with some margin
to concentrate the measure on the marked state, and $J(3,1)$ simply
does not have that margin; the graph is $K_3$, so its spectral gap is
$\Theta(1)$ and there is no slow direction for the QPE to discriminate
$|E\rangle$ from the rest of the spectrum. All downstream techniques
tried in Section~\ref{sec:mod} (symmetric Ref~B, inverse QFT, $2^j$
scaling, $\delta$ clamp) fix genuine bugs but cannot manufacture a gap
that is not there. The third cause is the set of \emph{genuine bugs} in the
code that are independent of the regime: Modification~1 (the
asymmetric Ref~B) and Modification~2 (the wrong cleanup block) are
real bugs and they have been fixed, and in a regime where MNRS has a
quadratic window to exploit they would be load-bearing; on $J(3,1)$,
however, there is no amplification window to begin with, which is why
fixing them is necessary for correctness but invisible on the final
distribution.

\subsection{Viable alternatives}

There are essentially two reasonable ways to move forward. The first
is to drop MNRS altogether and run a \emph{direct Grover search} on
the vertex register: for $N=\binom{n}{k}$ small (up to a few dozen)
one can index the admissible subsets on $\lceil\log_2 N\rceil$ qubits,
use an oracle that checks $\sum_{i\in S}x_i=p$, and apply
$\lfloor \tfrac{\pi}{4}\sqrt{N}\rfloor$ iterations with the standard
reflection around the uniform superposition. This works where MNRS
fails on toy instances because the reflection around the mean is
direct and does not depend on a QPE block that needs several qubits
of precision to act correctly. What we give up is the whole point of
the paper: we lose the quantum-walk speed-up and only get Grover's
asymptotic behaviour. The second alternative is to build a
\emph{simplified MNRS with a compact encoding}: for a fixed instance
like $(5,2)$ or $(6,3)$ one can drop the Dicke register, drop the
double cover $S_0$ and the $T$ copies, and initialise $|S\rangle$
directly on a single register of $\lceil\log_2 N\rceil$ qubits; the
QPE block can be replaced by an explicit reflection built from the
analytical eigenvectors of the walk operator on small Johnson graphs,
and the coin reduced to a single qubit when $k=1$. The resulting
circuit uses roughly 8--14 qubits and is simulable on
\texttt{PyLinalg} in minutes instead of hours. This is no longer a
faithful reproduction of the paper but a stripped-down variant whose
purpose is to demonstrate the amplification \emph{qualitatively}
while keeping the walk structure intact.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Closing remark.} The MNRS pipeline of the paper is
algorithmically correct once the two localised bugs in
Modifications~1 and~2 are removed (the fixed version lives in
\texttt{code\_updated/}), but it cannot be executed \emph{in a
meaningful way} on the toy instances $J(3,1)$ and $J(4,2)$, because
those instances sit outside the regime $\delta<1$ for which MNRS is
designed. The paper itself is explicit about this: its complexity
analysis targets the cryptographic scale ($n\ge 25$ for lower T-count,
$n\ge 2^{13}$ for lower T-depth) on fault-tolerant hardware, and notes
that the $n\log_2 n$ qubit overhead ``significantly limits the
feasibility of classical simulation''. The two realistic ways forward
are therefore to replace MNRS by a direct Grover search on a compact
vertex register, or to build a stripped-down MNRS variant on a single
register and an exact reflection, keeping the walk structure but
giving up the one-to-one correspondence with the paper.
